Five Expanded Core Theses

1. Miscommunication is a structural failure, not a personal flaw.

Current digital systems encode content, not context. The result is systemic friction, especially for neurodivergent, cross-cultural, and remote communicators. Communication breaks not because people are broken—but because the infrastructure is blind to meaning.

2. Emotional labor is the invisible cost of digital survival.

Miscommunication imposes a disproportionate cognitive and emotional tax on those whose communication styles deviate from dominant norms. This labor is uncompensated, unacknowledged, and structurally required in current platforms.

3. Clarity can be protocolized without compromising authenticity.

Human nuance is not too messy to model—it’s just been ignored in design. By encoding and sharing user-declared context vectors, we can scaffold mutual understanding without enforcing uniformity.

4. Consent-based AI can offer interpretive nudges that reduce harm.

By processing on-device and offering perspective previews (not corrections), assistive AI can illuminate potential misreads before messages are sent—building trust and preserving autonomy.

5. Protocol-layer solutions can shift communication equity.

Instead of teaching marginalized users to navigate dominant norms, Schism proposes flipping the model: embedding adaptive, interpretable, and equitable infrastructure into the systems themselves.
